Euler's Theorem

Mathematicians always try to make their language more effective. They usually call a lot of different things by one word.

Look at three items on the picture below. You may imagine the left of them as heavy iron balls connected by cords. The middle one looks like channels welded together. And may be the right item is a box made of paper, wood, or plastic. All the three have the same name for Mathematicians. They call each one A Cube.

8 vertices 12 edges 6 faces

Each of the Cubes has 8 vertices, 12 edges and 6 faces. It means that you may construct the model of the Cube connecting 8 balls with cords, or welding 12 channels, or assembling 6 square sheets of paper side by side.

The Cube is the simplest Polyhedron. You can imagine an arbitrary polyhedron as a solid with plane faces. For example, a sculptor, who makes his work chopping off the stone, actually makes a beautiful polyhedron. What is a mathematical definition for beauty? This question was very important for the classical Greece mathematics, which tried to build a perfect and flawless picture of our world.

The Classics searched for beauty everywhere. They searched the beauty for their bodies in Olimpiads, they searched the beauty of their speech in the Senate, they searched the beauty of the nature in their philosophy. Mathematics was not an exception. The Creator of the Geometry, the famous Euclid, completes his last work with the list of the most invariant, or symmetric, polyhedra.

Here they are: a tetrahedron, a cube (hexahedron), an octahedron, an icosahedron, a dodecahedron. Each of them has equal faces, edges, and angles. You can see these polyhedra in the pictures below.
